The Problem

FINRA's DXT initiative was stalling — not because investigators lacked tools, but because every team was using different ones, with no shared view of what was happening.

The mandate from FINRA's Digital Experience Transformation initiative was clear: address issues faster. But the slowness wasn't a workflow efficiency problem. Research revealed that different investigative teams were using entirely different products to track the same cases — case management in one system, surveillance data in another, status updates in Excel. There was no shared source of truth for the status of any active investigation.

Teams were working in silos. An investigator in one group had no visibility into what another group had already found. Duplicate work, missed signals, and delayed escalations weren't failures of effort — they were the inevitable result of a fragmented toolset with no connective tissue between teams.

As part of FINRA's Digital Experience Transformation (DXT), I helped design the Financial Crimes Investigation Dashboard — a unified, widget-based investigative workspace built on FINRA's Gateway platform, backed by AWS cloud infrastructure and Angular frontends with .NET microservices.

What research actually found

The investigators didn't need faster tools. They needed one place where every team could see the same thing at the same time.

The presenting problem was speed — the DXT initiative was focused on resolving issues faster. But contextual inquiry across investigative teams revealed the real blocker: no shared source of truth. Status lived in different systems depending on which team you asked. A unified dashboard wasn't the obvious solution at the start of the project — it was the conclusion the research led to. Once that structural root cause was clear, the design direction followed directly.

What teams reported

Slowness — investigations taking too long to resolve

Every team pointed to the same symptom: things moved too slowly. Escalations were delayed. Status was unclear. Critical signals weren't reaching the right people fast enough. The instinct was to optimize each team's individual workflow.

What research revealed

Silos — each team operating in its own disconnected system

The actual cause was structural: different teams used different tools, spoke different status languages, and had no shared view of case state. Optimizing individual workflows would have made each silo faster in isolation — while leaving the cross-team coordination problem completely unsolved.

Process

Five phases — from contextual inquiry and workflow mapping to interaction fidelity and fast validation.

Partnered with FINRA investigators, product, and engineering to understand the full investigative workflow, map decision points and friction, and design a modular workspace that surfaced the right signal at the right moment.

01
Contextual Inquiry — Uncovering the Structural Root Cause
Conducted structured contextual inquiry across investigative teams with a mandate to understand why issue resolution was slow under the DXT initiative. Teams described slowness, missed escalations, and unclear status — but the research task was finding why. What emerged wasn't a workflow efficiency problem: each team was using different products to track the same cases, with no shared source of truth. Status meant different things depending on who you asked. The silo structure was the root cause. That finding reframed the entire design direction — from optimizing individual team workflows to designing a single shared workspace that gave every team visibility into the same case state simultaneously.
02
Workflow Architecture & Investigative Mapping
Built a detailed Matter Sequence workflow map — tracing the full investigative lifecycle from case intake through resolution. Identified branching decision points, data dependencies, and the moments where investigators needed consolidated context most urgently.
03
Structured Collaboration & Widget Architecture
Ran cross-functional sessions to align on the widget-based dashboard model — defining which investigative functions became modular widgets, how they connected, and how the Gateway platform's component architecture would support customizable investigator workspaces.
04
Interaction Fidelity & Prototyping
Built high-fidelity prototypes covering the full investigator journey — case overview, data browsing, cross-reference navigation, alert triage, and collaborative annotation. Prototypes were detailed enough to test real investigator behavior, not just surface impressions.
05
Fast Validation & Visual Clarity
Ran task-based usability sessions with FINRA investigators — presenting high-fidelity prototypes and assigning realistic investigation scenarios to observe how quickly investigators could locate case status, triage alerts, and navigate between widgets under time pressure. Sessions validated triage logic, information hierarchy, and widget layout decisions, and directly drove iteration on visual clarity — density adjustments, color coding for status differentiation, and progressive disclosure thresholds — until the dashboard could be read accurately at speed.

Widget-based architecture

Modular by design — customizable by investigator

The dashboard was built on FINRA Gateway's widget framework — each investigative function (case summary, alert feed, data cross-reference, timeline) existed as an independent, interchangeable component. Investigators could configure their workspace around their specific workflow without losing system coherence.

Research-driven decisions

Every priority traceable to investigator evidence

The contextual inquiry data directly drove feature prioritization, widget hierarchy, and information density decisions. Nothing in the final design was arbitrary — every choice mapped back to a documented investigator need or workflow constraint.

"The most important design decision wasn't visual — it was defining which investigative functions needed to live together in the same view, and which ones could stay a click away."

Outcomes

A unified investigative workspace — replacing fragmented tools with a governed, widget-based platform built for financial crimes work.

Structural root cause identified and addressed — research revealed that siloed tools with no shared case status were the actual blocker to DXT's speed mandate, reframing the solution from workflow optimization to a unified shared workspace.
Single shared source of truth established — for the first time, every investigative team could see the same case status in the same place, eliminating the cross-team coordination failures that had been slowing resolution times.
Consolidated 6+ investigative tools into a single, configurable workspace — eliminating the context switching that slowed investigator decision-making.
Widget-based architecture adopted across FINRA Gateway — modular components that investigators could configure around their specific workflow needs.
Research-validated design decisions — every feature priority, widget hierarchy, and density choice traceable to documented investigator evidence from contextual inquiry.
Faster alert triage and case review — structured risk indicators and progressive disclosure replaced manual cross-referencing across disconnected tools.
AWS cloud-native scalability — the dashboard architecture was built to scale with FINRA's real-time data processing requirements, not against them.
Design system contribution — investigative widget patterns contributed to FINRA Gateway's centralized component library, ensuring consistency across the consolidated platform.

What I'd do differently

Start widget governance conversations with engineering earlier. The modular architecture worked well, but some widget interaction patterns — particularly cross-widget data sharing and state persistence — weren't fully defined until mid-build. A joint design-engineering session on widget communication contracts at the start of the project would have prevented the rework that came later. When the component architecture is this central to the product, the technical model and the design model need to be designed together from day one.
