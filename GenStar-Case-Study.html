The Problem

Underwriters working across fragmented tools, Excel sheets, and disconnected screens — with key signals buried and decisions slowing down.

General Star's underwriting teams were slowed by fragmented tools, inconsistent UI patterns, and manual processes that created friction at every step. Key signals — weather, market shifts, and exposure context — were buried or missing entirely across disconnected screens. The result was slow decisions, inconsistent reviews, and high operational risk.

I designed the first unified underwriting dashboard for the GENESIS G2 intranet, streamlining workflows, embedding reusable Material components, and establishing a governed system that scaled across teams and frameworks.

The core tension

Underwriters needed real-time signal visibility to make faster, more confident decisions — but the tools they had were built for data entry, not decision support.

Every signal an underwriter needed — weather exposure, market conditions, risk triage indicators — lived in a different tool or spreadsheet. Consolidating that into a single, role-based workspace meant rethinking both the information architecture and the component system that would deliver it.

My Role

Led the strategic UX overhaul of General Star's underwriting dashboard on the GENESIS G2 platform.

What I owned
Full UX scope — discovery, comparative audit, risk triage logic, dashboard architecture, prototyping, Material-aligned component design, and implementation support for React, Angular, AG Grid, and .NET.
The strategic layer
Defined risk logic and streamlined decision paths — not just UI patterns. The design had to reflect how underwriters actually think through risk, not just how data was stored in the system.
The platform constraint
GENESIS G2 ran React, Angular, and .NET modules with AG Grid for data-heavy views. Every component had to be Material-aligned, implementation-ready, and consumable across all frameworks without rework.
Process

Four phases — from discovery and competitive audit to architecture, prototyping, and engineering handoff.

Partnered with underwriting leadership, product, and engineering to architect a role-based workflow that unified fragmented data, clarified triage logic, and reduced decision friction across multi-state operations.

01
Discovery & Stakeholder Alignment
Interviewed underwriting leads and audited legacy tools to identify workflow gaps, lookup friction, and inconsistent UI patterns. Mapped how decisions were actually being made versus how the system expected them to happen.
02
Comparative Audit & Benchmarking
Evaluated Addepar, FactSet, Jorne, and internal tools to benchmark clarity, density, and triage behavior — establishing a clear bar for what a best-in-class underwriting workspace should deliver at a glance.
03
UX Architecture & Prototyping
Designed role-based dashboard flows with progressive disclosure, real-time signal widgets, and structured risk indicators. Prototyped the full decision-support workspace before any component work began.
04
Design System & Engineering Support
Built tokenized Material components and delivered implementation-ready specs aligned to React, Angular, AG Grid, and .NET — ensuring the design system worked within GENESIS G2's full framework stack without requiring a platform rewrite.
Risk triage logic

Designed for how underwriters think, not how data is stored

The dashboard architecture was built around risk triage logic — surfacing the most decision-relevant signals first, with progressive disclosure for deeper context. Role-based views ensured each underwriter saw what they needed without noise.

Material + AG Grid

Implementation-ready for React & Angular

Every component was Material-aligned and delivered as engineering-ready specs consumable by both React and Angular modules on GENESIS G2 — with AG Grid integration for data-dense underwriting views.

What I'd do differently

Involve underwriting leads earlier in the component review process — not just discovery. The risk triage logic and dashboard hierarchy were well-validated, but some component-level decisions (particularly around AG Grid density and progressive disclosure thresholds) required multiple rounds of revision once real underwriting data was loaded. Earlier data-realistic testing would have surfaced those edge cases before engineering handoff.
