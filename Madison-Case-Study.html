The Problem

Staffing coordinators managing contacts across fragmented views with no unified relationship model — and no consistent way to track what mattered.

Madison Resources' Navigator CRM had grown organically — contact data was spread across disconnected views, relationship context was buried or missing, and coordinators were working around the system rather than through it. Data entry was inconsistent, relationship tracking was manual, and there was no clear information hierarchy to guide decision-making.

The goal was to design a unified contact relationship management experience inside Navigator — built on .NET Blazor and powered by the Gator Design System established earlier in the engagement.

The core tension

Staffing coordinators needed to see the full relationship context of every contact — but the system was built for data storage, not relationship visibility.

Every contact existed in isolation. There was no way to quickly understand who a contact was connected to, what their status was across active placements, or where they sat in the coordinator's workflow. Surfacing that context — without overwhelming the interface — required a careful rethinking of information architecture, not just visual design.

Led UX design for Navigator's contact relationship system — from information architecture through .NET Blazor component delivery.

Gator DS

Process

From contact taxonomy and relationship mapping to component architecture and .NET Blazor handoff.

Partnered with product and engineering to untangle Navigator's contact model, define a clear relationship hierarchy, and design a CRM experience that gave coordinators visibility into what mattered — without adding complexity.

01
Discovery & Understanding the Problem
Audited existing Navigator contact views, interviewed staffing coordinators, and mapped where the current system created friction — missed relationship context, redundant data entry, inconsistent status tracking across placements.
02
Contact Taxonomy & Information Architecture
Defined the contact data model — entity types, relationship categories, status hierarchies, and the connections between contacts, companies, and active placements. Built the taxonomy that would anchor every view in the system.
03
Relationship Mapping & Flow Architecture
Designed the relationship model — how contacts connected to companies, placements, and coordinators — and translated that into navigable flows. Progressive disclosure ensured depth was available without overwhelming the primary view.
04
Card-Based Component Architecture
Designed a card-based system using Gator DS components — contact cards, relationship panels, status indicators, and command patterns — that gave coordinators scannable, action-ready views of every contact and their context.
05
UX Architecture & Prototyping
Built end-to-end prototypes covering contact search, profile views, relationship navigation, and data entry flows — validated with coordinators before engineering handoff.
06
.NET Blazor Implementation Support
Delivered Gator-aligned, implementation-ready specs for .NET Blazor — covering component states, interaction patterns, data binding expectations, and edge cases. Stayed in through integration to close the gap between design and shipped behavior.
Relationship visibility

Contact context at a glance

The core design challenge was surfacing relationship depth — who a contact was connected to, their placement history, and their current status — without requiring coordinators to navigate across multiple views. A progressive disclosure model solved it.

Gator DS in practice

Governed components, faster delivery

Using Gator DS meant every pattern decision was grounded in an established system — card layouts, filter patterns, status badges, and command surfaces were all pre-governed. Engineering could move faster because the design was already consistent and documented.

Blazor component design

Designed for how Blazor components actually work

Specs included Blazor-specific layout patterns, component state definitions, parameter bindings, and responsive behavior — not just visual mockups. This meant developers had everything they needed to implement accurately in Blazor without back-and-forth on interaction gaps.

Responsive & pixel-perfect

Consistent across desktop, tablet & mobile

Navigator CRM was designed with a responsive-first approach — every component specified for desktop, tablet, and mobile breakpoints. Figma files included annotated specs, component variants, and handoff-ready assets aligned to Blazor's rendering constraints.

"Designing on top of a system you built yourself means you can move faster — and make better decisions. Gator wasn't just a library, it was institutional knowledge embedded in components."

What I'd do differently

Involve .NET Blazor engineers earlier in the component review cycle. The Gator system translated well into Blazor, but some interaction patterns — particularly around data binding for relationship panels and progressive disclosure triggers — required revision once the engineering team flagged server-side rendering constraints. Earlier joint reviews between design and Blazor engineers would have caught those gaps before they became handoff friction.
