The Problem

EcoStruxure subscription activation was a black box — complex dependencies, unclear states, and no coherent management experience for users.

Schneider Electric's EcoStruxure platform — an IoT-enabled architecture connecting building, grid, and industrial operations — had a subscription and activation model that had grown too complex for users to navigate confidently. Feature dependencies were invisible, activation logic was inconsistent across territories, and users had no reliable way to understand what they owned, what was active, or what was shared.

I was brought in to untangle that complexity — mapping the full activation model, validating against real user needs through card-sort research, and designing a subscription management experience that made the invisible visible.

The core tension

EcoStruxure's subscription model was technically accurate — but completely unintelligible to the users responsible for managing it.

The underlying logic was sound: features had dependencies, subscriptions had sharing rules, activation followed a defined sequence. But none of that was surfaced in a way users could understand or act on. The design challenge wasn't simplifying the system — it was making the system's complexity legible without removing the control users needed.

My Role

Led UX research and design for EcoStruxure subscription management — from activation mapping through wireframes and final UI.

What I owned
Full scope — activation complexity mapping, subscription purchase logic, workflow dependency visualization, card-sort research, wireframes, development alignment, and final UI design for the subscription management interface.
The platform context
EcoStruxure runs on .NET Framework and Azure IoT infrastructure. The SmartConnector middleware layer — built in C# and .NET — handles subscription logic and feature activation. Design specs had to account for how that backend model surfaced in the UI.
The research depth
Card interviews across territory and subscription personas — combining research opinions and use cases to validate the activation model against real user mental models before any wireframe work began.
Process

Five phases — from activation mapping and dependency visualization to card research, wireframes, and final UI.

Partnered with product and engineering to fully map EcoStruxure's subscription activation model, validate against real user needs, and design an interface that gave users clear visibility and control — without requiring them to understand the underlying technical architecture.

01
Understanding the Problem & Activation Mapping
Audited the existing subscription experience, interviewed product and engineering stakeholders, and began mapping the full activation complexity — feature dependencies, sharing rules, territory-specific logic, and the sequences users had to navigate to get from purchase to active state.
02
Subscription Purchase — Mapping Activation Logic
Mapped the full subscription purchase and activation logic — tracing every path from initial purchase through feature activation, including the branching conditions, dependency gates, and failure states that users encountered. Built the logic map that became the shared reference for design and engineering.
03
Workflow Architecture — Visualizing Sharing Dependencies
Created a dependency visualization — mapping how subscriptions shared features across users, territories, and product tiers. The color-coded architecture diagram made the sharing model legible for the first time, both for the design process and for stakeholder alignment.
04
Card Interviews — Territory & Subscription Personas
Ran structured card-sort interviews across territory and subscription personas — combining research opinions and real use cases to validate how users understood subscription boundaries, feature ownership, and sharing expectations. Findings directly shaped the information architecture of the management interface.
05
Wireframes to Development Alignment & Final UI
Designed wireframes covering the full subscription management workflow — activation, sharing, state visibility, and management actions — and aligned them directly with the .NET/.NET Framework backend model. Iterated through to final UI, including the dark-themed subscription visibility and sharing management screens.

.NET & Azure IoT context

Designing for a .NET-backed activation model

EcoStruxure's subscription logic runs on .NET Framework, with the SmartConnector middleware built in C# handling feature activation and sharing rules. Design specs were written with that backend model in mind — ensuring UI states mapped accurately to the activation sequences the .NET layer could actually produce.

From complexity to clarity

Making the invisible visible

The final UI gave users — for the first time — a clear view of their subscription state, active features, sharing relationships, and activation status. The dark-themed interface was designed for density and scannability, appropriate for the operations-focused EcoStruxure user context.

"The most valuable deliverable wasn't the UI — it was the activation logic map. Once everyone could see the full model, the right design decisions became obvious."

Outcomes

A legible subscription management experience — built on a fully mapped activation model and validated against real user needs.

Full activation model documented — the first complete map of EcoStruxure's subscription logic, shared across design, product, and engineering as a single source of truth.
Sharing dependency architecture visualized — made the feature sharing model legible for users and stakeholders, directly enabling better design decisions.

What I'd do differently

Run the card interviews before completing the activation logic map — not after. I mapped the full technical activation model first, which was necessary for design credibility, but the card research then revealed that users had fundamentally different mental models for some of the concepts I'd been designing around. Doing them in parallel would have let user language shape the map from the start, rather than requiring me to reconcile two separate models after both were complete.
