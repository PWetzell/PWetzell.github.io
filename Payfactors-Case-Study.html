Context

The first UX designer at a compensation analytics startup — building the product foundation before a single line of code.

As the first UX/UI designer at Payfactors, I built the foundation of a compensation analytics platform that transformed complex salary benchmarking into a clear, actionable workflow for HR teams. My Axure high-fidelity prototypes became the centerpiece of monthly product preview videos led by the Head of Product — showcasing new features and upcoming tools before any code was written.

These prototypes were critical for aligning stakeholders, validating functionality, and avoiding costly development missteps. I partnered across product, marketing, and engineering to drive feature releases and secure adoption — ultimately contributing to the platform's acquisition by PayScale, where my original interfaces continue to support compensation strategy at scale.

The strategic impact

Prototypes that sold the product before it existed — and survived the acquisition.

At an early-stage startup, design isn't just UX — it's product strategy made visible. The Axure prototypes I built weren't throwaway artifacts. They were the demos that closed stakeholders, the reference point engineering built from, and the interfaces that, after PayScale acquired Payfactors in 2021, continued running at scale with the combined platform's user base.

The platform stack
Payfactors ran on C# and .NET Framework (moving toward .NET Core), hosted on Microsoft Azure with SQL Server for compensation datasets. The frontend used Angular and React for analytics dashboards and the QuickPrice interactive views.
The prototyping approach
High-fidelity Axure prototypes — detailed enough to demo as working product. Each prototype was recorded into a product preview video presented monthly by the Head of Product. Design was the first deliverable, not the last.
The acquisition context
PayScale acquired Payfactors in 2021, relaunching as a unified compensation engine combining Payfactors' datasets with PayScale's algorithms. The interfaces I designed continued to support compensation strategy across the merged platform.

"Being the first UX designer means every pattern you establish becomes the product's design language. There's no system to inherit — you build the one that ships."

01 QuickPrice 7:36
Problem

HR professionals needed a fast, reliable way to benchmark compensation across roles, industries, and geographies — but existing tools were fragmented and difficult to navigate. There was no single place to get to a reliable salary number quickly.

Outcome
  • Delivered intuitive data visualizations to support job comparisons and salary benchmarking
  • Designed vertical navigation bars to guide users through pricing workflows
  • Created a fast, scannable interface that reduced time-to-insight for compensation decisions
02 Peer Association 5:29
Problem

Users struggled to associate jobs across peer organizations due to cluttered interfaces and a lack of filtering logic. Finding relevant peer comparisons required too much manual effort and produced inconsistent results.

Outcome
  • Introduced a two-column layout for quick comparison between company and peer jobs
  • Filtered out salary-associated jobs to improve productivity and reduce cognitive load
  • Gave users a clear, structured view of peer relationships that scaled across large job libraries
03 Job Ranges 9:38
Problem

Compensation analysts needed a flexible way to model job ranges across peer groups, but the interface lacked clarity and filtering logic — making it difficult to configure ranges with confidence or compare them across peer sets efficiently.

Outcome
  • Leveraged the two-column layout to support quick comparisons and model configuration
  • Filtered out unassociated jobs to streamline workflows and reduce manual effort
  • Enabled analysts to model and validate job ranges across peer groups in a single, structured view

What I'd do differently

Establish a lightweight design system earlier. As the only designer, every new feature I built started from scratch — components, patterns, and interaction conventions were consistent because I was consistent, not because there was a documented system. That worked at startup scale, but as the product grew and engineering teams expanded, the lack of a formal component library created friction. The Axure prototypes were detailed enough to serve as the de facto reference, but an actual system would have accelerated handoff and protected consistency post-acquisition.
