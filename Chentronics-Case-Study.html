The Problem

Field technicians diagnosing industrial flame sensors with manual checks, inconsistent logs, and trial-and-error.

Chentronics, a leader in industrial flame detection, needed a mobile application to support technicians diagnosing sensor health in the field. With the iScan® 3+ launch approaching and competitors already offering mobile tools, the absence of a mobile diagnostic experience created operational risk, competitive pressure, and workflow friction.

The core tension

Complex hardware signals needed to be translated into clear, actionable diagnostic steps — in real-time, in the field, under pressure.

Technicians were relying on manual checks, inconsistent hardware logs, and trial-and-error to diagnose flame sensor health. Offline signals were buried in hardware readouts, troubleshooting steps varied across technicians, and there was no clear visibility into sensor health during high-risk industrial operations.

My Role

Led the UX effort to translate complex hardware behavior into a clear, actionable diagnostic experience.

What I owned
End-to-end UX — from field workflow research and fault-state modeling to interaction design, hardware-integrated prototyping, and implementation support across iOS and Android.
Mobile-first for real field conditions
Every screen was designed mobile-first — thumb reach zones, glove-friendly tap targets, high-contrast states for outdoor lighting, and single-hand navigation. The experience had to work on a phone in a mechanical bay, not just in a design review. React Native's single codebase meant every layout decision had to hold across iOS and Android screen sizes simultaneously.
The constraint
Hardware signals are complex and non-linear. Every diagnostic decision had to account for real sensor behavior — not simplified abstractions. And it all had to be legible on a small screen, under pressure, in the field. Accuracy and mobile usability were both non-negotiable.
Process

Five phases — from hardware audit to live prototype validation with real sensor signals.

01
Field Workflow & Hardware Audit
Conducted contextual inquiry sessions with field technicians — observing diagnostic workflows in situ, reviewing hardware logs, and mapping the end-to-end process to classify friction and failure points. Structured observations surfaced the specific moments where manual steps created the highest risk and where technicians were compensating for missing tool support.
02
Fault-State Modeling & Signal Mapping
Collaborated with firmware engineers to translate raw sensor data into modeled states — thresholds, timeouts, and repair indicators. Created a shared fault-state taxonomy used across design and engineering.
03
Diagnostic Flow Architecture
Designed structured troubleshooting paths that surfaced root causes, recommended actions, and real-time sensor feedback at every decision point — reducing ambiguity for technicians in the field.
04
UI Design & Interaction Patterns
Created a clear, readable interface with sensor health hierarchy, color-coded states, and guided steps aligned to technician behavior. Designed for glanceability under industrial conditions. Figma files included annotated spacing on an 8pt grid, component state matrices for every diagnostic state, and mobile breakpoint specs covering the range of iOS and Android screen sizes in the field technician device inventory.
05
Hardware-Integrated Prototype Validation
Ran task-based usability sessions with technicians using prototypes connected to real iScan® 3+ hardware signals via engineering test rigs — validating timing, state transition accuracy, and diagnostic flow clarity under conditions that matched actual field use. Tasks included fault identification, filter navigation, and repair confirmation sequences. Findings directly drove iteration on information hierarchy and state labeling before final build.

Fault-state taxonomy

Shared language across design & engineering

Rather than designing around assumed states, I built a shared fault taxonomy with firmware engineers — ensuring every UI state mapped directly to a real hardware condition. No guesswork, no abstraction gaps.

Implementation support

Stayed in until it shipped correctly

Uncovered layout inconsistencies during integration and refined interactions based on real-world feedback from engineering test sessions — not just design reviews.

Responsive across every screen size

Designed for the full device spectrum — not just one phone

React Native's single codebase meant layouts had to hold across iOS and Android, across small and large screen sizes, and across different OS rendering behaviors. Every component was specified for the smallest supported screen first, then validated on larger devices — a genuine mobile-first approach, not a desktop layout squeezed down.

Field-condition design constraints

Designed for how and where the app actually gets used

Glove-friendly tap targets (minimum 48px), high-contrast states for outdoor lighting, single-hand navigation patterns, and reduced cognitive load for time-pressured decisions. The screen-size work was inseparable from the environmental constraints — both shaped every layout decision.

Outcomes

A scalable diagnostic framework — and measurably faster fault resolution in the field.

30% faster fault identification during field diagnostics — reduced time-to-resolution for technicians working on industrial installations.
Reduced technician guesswork through guided troubleshooting flows — replacing manual cross-referencing with clear, step-by-step repair paths.
Clear visibility into sensor health and failure modes — technicians could immediately understand device state without consulting hardware manuals.
Improved engineering confidence in data accuracy and UI behavior through accurate sensor-state mapping and hardware-validated interactions.
Scalable diagnostic patterns reused across additional hardware tools — the framework became the foundation for future Chentronics mobile products.
Competitive parity achieved ahead of iScan® 3+ launch — closing the gap with competitors already offering mobile diagnostic experiences.

What I'd do differently

Get hardware test rigs in front of the design earlier. The most valuable feedback came when we tested the prototype against real sensor signals — but that happened late in the process. Earlier hardware-integrated testing would have caught state-transition edge cases before they became engineering rework. When hardware is the source of truth, it needs to be in the room from day one.
